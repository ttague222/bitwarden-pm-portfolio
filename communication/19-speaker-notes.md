# Speaker Notes
### Per-slide delivery notes for the 25-minute assessment presentation

*Register: measured, specific, first-person. The deck asserts; the notes carry the evidence and the humility. Timeboxes total ~24 minutes speaking.*

---

**S1 (0:45).** Say the method sentence slowly, it's the credibility deposit: "Everything you'll see is public data, fetched in July, with confidence levels stated. Where I couldn't know something, I say so."

**S2 (0:45).** Don't oversell. "Hundreds of people can tell you they're evidence-driven. I'd rather show you what it looks like when evidence costs me my favorite idea. That's slide 14."

**S3 (1:30).** Sincerity check: this must not sound like flattery-before-critique. Anchor: "The 8-day GPL relicense told me more about this company than any feature. You re-architected repositories in a week to protect a principle. That's the asset every recommendation here spends carefully."

**S4 (1:30).** Walk the chart left to right. Land: "I'm not here to propose a new strategy. The shipping record shows you have one. I'm here to make five of its bets more likely to pay."

**S5 (1:30).** Let the vote numbers breathe; they're the emotional core of the community's patience. "The community has learned that voting works, eventually. 'Eventually' is the grievance, and 2026 is the year it visibly started to shorten. That momentum is worth protecting."

**S6 (1:30).** The reframe slide. "I went in asking 'what features do users want?' The engagement data answered a different question: what users *guard*. Redesigns, regressions, licensing. Once you see the community as a governance body, several product decisions look different."

**S7 (2:00).** The pivot point of the whole talk, slow down. "Fifty-one votes to dismiss a warning. Forty to explain it. Thirteen thousand views asking 'why is this flagged?' Your own users wrote a product spec in the feature-request forum. That's not a complaint; that's a gift."

**S8 (2:00).** One line per opportunity, no more, the briefs exist. Signal depth, not coverage: "Each of these has a full discovery brief: evidence, hypotheses, experiments, unknowns, and what internal data would change my mind." Then point at the persona strip: "And everything downstream is designed against four evidence-derived personas, because one guidance surface has four different correct behaviors. A checklist that is essential for the person who just imported 400 items is noise for the person who opens the app to grab one credential. The personas are how the program stays respectful for both."

**S9 (1:30).** Pre-empt the scoring skepticism yourself: "Weighted scores are judgment made inspectable, not math. If your internal numbers say my demand column is wrong, the table recalculates in front of you, that's what it's for."

**S10 (2:00).** The ask crystallizes here. "Four phases, in an order the research chose: you can't extend guidance from a surface users resent, so repair comes first. The January renewal cohort turns this from a nice-to-have into a dated commitment."

**S11 (2:00).** The differentiation argument, make the competitive asymmetry explicit: "1Password cannot publish Watchtower's rules without changing their business. You publish your cryptography and got a better audit for it. Extending show-your-work from code to advice is the most Bitwarden-shaped move available."

**S12 (1:30).** Preempt the telemetry objection before anyone raises it: "Every metric has a stated fallback tier that respects the privacy posture, server-visible facts, cohort outcomes, opt-in aggregates. Slower reads, honest intervals. The posture is a constraint I designed within, not around."

**S13 (1:30).** This is the humility slide with teeth, it doubles as "what I'd actually do if you hired me." Read two rows of the table aloud, not all seven.

**S14 (2:00).** Tell it as a story, 3 beats: "I came in with a flagship concept, a security coach for mobile. Then the research: you shipped coaching in December. Then the community's response taught me what my concept had gotten wrong, I'd designed the guidance; I hadn't designed the *consent*. The final recommendation is better than my first idea, and the delta is the point."

**S15 (2:00).** Invite the strongest objection first: "The best argument against my recommendation is opportunity #1, reliability is the bigger lever. I concluded it's a program for existing owners, not a charter for a new hire. If you tell me the autofill team wants a PM, I'll update in this meeting."

**S16 (0:45).** Close on the date, not the vision: it makes the whole thing concrete and accountable. Then stop talking.

---

**Q&A preparation (the five likely challenges):**
1. *"Our telemetry won't support this."* → Metrics/14 degraded path; week-one tier conversation with security; cohort outcomes carry the decision weight.
2. *"The internal fix already covers Phase 1."* → Best case. The program's differentiated value is Phases 2–4; Phase 1 becomes a join, not a build.
3. *"An RFC on our scoring invites a pile-on."* → The pile-on is happening anyway in the feature-request forum; the RFC moves it into a structure where adopting the best criticism is the story. Pre-registered success criteria keep it honest.
4. *"Why should an external candidate's read outweigh our roadmap?"* → It shouldn't. Slide 13 is the list of internal facts that would change my ranking; the artifact demonstrates the *process*, and the process defers to better data.
5. *"Isn't this just 1Password's Watchtower?"* → Watchtower is the table stakes; the published, community-governed methodology is the part Watchtower can't do. Match the bar, then move it where only Bitwarden can follow.
